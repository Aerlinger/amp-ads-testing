Milos Raonic upsets Roger Federer, will face Andy Murray in Wimbledon men’s final

By Chuck Culpepper

July 8, 2016 at 2:10 PM

WIMBLEDON, England — The long-rising tennis career of the 25-year-old Canadian Milos Raonic found an apex Friday, when he stood in fifth-set tension opposite Roger Federer and wrung his first Grand Slam final berth.

Raonic’s 6-3, 6-7 (3), 4-6, 7-5, 6-3 win included a riveting 11-shot rally that sent both players flying around to close the fourth game of that fifth set. When Raonic won that break point by ripping a cross-court, forehand passing shot, he rode his own freight-train serve from there to a spot as an underdog villain in Sunday’s final.

Then, he will play Andy Murray, the Scottish second-ranked player who in 2013 because the first British Wimbledon men’s singles champion since Fred Perry in 1936. Murray committed just nine unforced errors in the second men’s semifinal, tearing through ninth-ranked Tomas Berdych 6-3, 6-3, 6-3, and reaching his 11th Grand Slam final — curiously, his first against a player ranked lower than himself.

“I am by no means done,” Raonic said after what he called an “incredible comeback.”

“He’s obviously playing the best grass-court tennis of his career,” Murray said on the BBC, ahead of a rematch of his taut final with Raonic on grass three weeks ago at Queen’s Club in London, where Murray won 6-7 (5), 6-4, 6-3.

As Raonic got his first grass-court win over a top-10 player, and became the first Canadian man in a Grand Slam final, Federer had to cope with a dejection unusually deep. At age almost-35, with nemesis Novak Djokovic cleared from the path in the third round, Federer played 10 sets within three days, withstood three match points against Marin Cilic in a five-set slog of a quarterfinal, and came brutally close to being the oldest Wimbledon finalist since a 39-year-old Ken Rosewall in 1974.

Worse, at a vital point in the 12th game of the fourth set, after leading 40-love, he double-faulted twice in a row, matching his aggregate double-fault total from the first five rounds of the tournament.

“I don’t know,” he said soon thereafter. “Something went wrong. I don’t know. I can’t believe I served a double-fault twice. Unexplainable for me, really, yeah. Very sad about that and angry at myself because never should I allow him to get out of that set that easily.

“I mean, he earned it. He deserved it at the end. But I helped him so much to get back into that game.”

By then, in that fourth set, Raonic had faced three break points — effective match points — two in the fifth game and one in the ninth. When he got to deuce after Federer’s double faults in the 5-6 game, he cashed in on his third set point. Federer got a short-ball return but mistakenly directed it into Raonic’s backhand corner, where Raonic waited and directed a cross-court pass that clinched the matter, 7-5.

For a good mid-afternoon while, Federer seemed ready to push on through, at a Wimbledon to which he arrived feeling “insecure,” he said, having missed the French Open while recovering from knee surgery and a back injury. When he surpassed the downer of losing four set points in the second set against Raonic, and won handily in the tiebreaker, then broke Raonic in the seventh game of the fourth set, his experience looked superior.

Raonic, in just his third Grand Slam semifinal, and his second against Federer within three years here, looked endangered, and told the BBC he was “really struggling.” Federer’s usual partisan crowd murmured in anticipation when Raonic faced the fourth-set break points. Victory seemed near. “So many opportunities were all around the fourth set,” he said. “. . . But somehow, I couldn’t get it done.”

By the fifth set, he had taken a rare spill in the fourth game — “I don’t slip a lot; I don’t ever fall down,” he said — and fell face-down in the grass. “I just felt not the same afterward,” Federer said. “Regardless, he played a great break point to get the break.”

Finally, five years after a splash when he reached the Australian Open fourth round as a qualifier, Raonic had a first-ever chance to clinch a Grand Slam semifinal with his serve. The 6-foot-5 born Yugoslav, whose parents immigrated from present-day Montenegro when he was 3, did so nervelessly at love: a service winner, a 136-mph ace, another service winner and a deep approach shot that a running Federer sent long.

The 17-time Grand Slam champion then hunted for encouragement. “The 10 sets I played in the last two matches really gives me the belief that I’m much, how do you say, match-fit or physically tougher than I thought I was,” Federer said. “I never thought I could do this before the tournament started. Actually, it’s very encouraging for the season, hopefully for the rest of my career.”

Chuck Culpepper covers national college sports for the Washington Post.
